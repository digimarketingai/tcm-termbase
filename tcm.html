The story of Chinese medicine's westward transmission began in 1688 when Louis XIV of France sent five Jesuit missionaries to China. These missionaries not only spread religion but also brought Chinese medical knowledge back to Europe. Polish missionary Michael Boym published "Flora Sinensis" in 1656, pioneering the westward transmission of Chinese medicinals.


However, early translations had numerous problems. Due to cultural differences and lack of professional knowledge, many translations were inaccurate or incomplete, leading to Western misunderstandings of TCM concepts. The theoretical system of Traditional Chinese Medicine is a unique medical theory with Yin-Yang and Five Elements theory as its philosophical foundation, the holistic concept as its guiding ideology, the physiology and pathology of Zang-Fu organs and meridians as its core, and pattern differentiation and treatment as its diagnostic and therapeutic characteristics. Yin-Yang theory holds that everything in the universe contains two aspectsâ€”Yin and Yangâ€”that are mutually opposing yet interdependent, and that the contradictory movement of Yin and Yang is the fundamental cause of the generation, development, transformation, and extinction of all things.


Five Elements theory holds that Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water are the five basic substances constituting the universe, among which exist relationships of mutual generation and mutual restriction. Mutual generation refers to one substance having the function of promoting, fostering, and nourishing another, in the sequence: Wood generates Fire, Fire generates Earth, Earth generates Metal, Metal generates Water, Water generates Wood. Mutual restriction refers to one substance having the function of inhibiting, constraining, and weakening another, in the sequence: Wood restricts Earth, Earth restricts Water, Water restricts Fire, Fire restricts Metal, Metal restricts Wood.


In TCM, the Five Elements correspond to the five Zang organs: Liver pertains to Wood, Heart pertains to Fire, Spleen pertains to Earth, Lung pertains to Metal, Kidney pertains to Water. This correspondence is used not only to explain physiological connections among Zang-Fu organs but also to analyze pathological changes and guide clinical treatment. For example, "Liver Wood overacting on Spleen Earth" explains the pathological mechanism of emotional disturbance causing digestive dysfunction, while "nourishing Water to moisten Wood" is a treatment method of tonifying Kidney Yin to treat Liver Yin deficiency.

Qi, Blood, and Body Fluids are the basic substances that constitute the human body and maintain its life activities. Qi is an extremely refined substance with great vitality that circulates ceaselessly within the body, being one of the basic substances that constitute and maintain human life activities. Based on different origins, distributions, and functions, Qi can be divided into four types: Yuan Qi (Primordial Qi), Zong Qi (Pectoral Qi), Ying Qi (Nutritive Qi), and Wei Qi (Defensive Qi). Yuan Qi is inherited from parents, being the most fundamental and important Qi of the body, stored in the Kidney and distributed throughout the body via the Triple Jiao. Zong Qi accumulates in the chest, formed by the combination of fresh air inhaled by the Lung and food essence transformed by the Spleen and Stomach. Ying Qi circulates within the vessels and is an important component of blood. Wei Qi circulates outside the vessels, functioning to warm and nourish Zang-Fu organs, moisten skin and body hair, and regulate the opening and closing of pores.


Blood is a red, nutrient-rich liquid substance that circulates within the vessels. Blood formation mainly derives from food essence transformed by the Spleen and Stomach, which transforms into Blood under the action of Heart and Lung. The main function of Blood is to nourish and moisten all Zang-Fu organs and tissues. Body Fluids is a general term for all normal liquids in the body, including internal fluids and normal secretions of all Zang-Fu organs and tissues. Although Jin and Ye share the same source, they differ in consistency: Jin is clear and thin with high fluidity, distributed to body surface skin, muscles, and orifices for moistening; Ye is thick and viscous with low fluidity, infused into joints, Zang-Fu organs, brain, and marrow for nourishment.


Close relationships exist among Qi, Blood, and Body Fluids. Qi can generate Blood, move Blood, and control Blood, while Blood can carry Qi and nourish Qi. Qi can generate fluids, move fluids, and control fluids, while fluids can carry Qi. Fluids and Blood share the same source, both transformed from food essence. Therefore, pathologically, Qi deficiency can cause Blood deficiency, and Blood deficiency can cause Qi deficiency; Qi stagnation can cause Blood stasis, and Blood stasis can cause Qi stagnation; fluid insufficiency can cause Blood dryness, and Blood dryness can cause fluid insufficiency. In clinical treatment, supplementing Qi and supplementing Blood, regulating Qi and activating Blood, generating fluids and nourishing Blood are often combined.

The main methods of TCM diagnosis are Inspection (Wang), Auscultation-Olfaction (Wen), Inquiry (Wen), and Palpation (Qie), collectively called the "Four Diagnostic Methods." Inspection involves the practitioner using visual observation to examine the patient's spirit, complexion, physical form, posture, tongue appearance, and excreta to understand the condition. Tongue diagnosis is particularly important, using observation of tongue body and coating changes to determine Zang-Fu deficiency or excess, Qi and Blood abundance or decline, pathogen nature, and disease progression. Pale tongue body usually indicates Qi and Blood deficiency; red or crimson tongue body indicates heat excess; white greasy coating indicates cold-dampness; yellow greasy coating indicates damp-heat.


Auscultation-Olfaction includes listening to sounds and smelling odors. Listening involves hearing the patient's speech, breathing, coughing, vomiting, hiccups, bowel sounds, etc. Smelling involves detecting odors from the patient's body and excreta. Inquiry is the method of understanding the condition through questioning the patient or accompanying persons. Inquiry content includes asking about cold and heat, sweating, head and body, urination and defecation, diet, chest and abdomen, ears and eyes, sleep, previous illnesses, and disease causes. Ming Dynasty physician Zhang Jingyue compiled the "Ten Questions Song," which remains an important reference for inquiry today.


Palpation includes pulse diagnosis and physical palpation. Pulse diagnosis involves the practitioner pressing the patient's artery with fingers to perceive pulse changes for disease diagnosis. The most commonly used method is Cun-Kou pulse diagnosis, performed at the radial artery of the wrist, divided into three positions: Cun, Guan, and Chi. Pulse elements include pulse position, rate, rhythm, shape, and strength. There are twenty-eight common pulse patterns, such as floating pulse, deep pulse, slow pulse, rapid pulse, deficient pulse, excess pulse, slippery pulse, choppy pulse, etc. Physical palpation involves the practitioner touching and pressing certain areas of the patient's body surface to understand the condition, including palpating the chest and abdomen, hands and feet, and acupoints.

Physiognomy (face reading) has a close relationship with TCM inspection, both judging a person's health and fortune through observing facial features. In TCM theory, different facial regions correspond to the Five Zang organs: the forehead pertains to the Heart, the nose pertains to the Spleen, the left cheek pertains to the Liver, the right cheek pertains to the Lung, and the chin pertains to the Kidney. Changes in facial complexion can reflect Zang-Fu pathology: pale complexion usually indicates Qi and Blood deficiency or Yang deficiency, flushed complexion indicates Yin deficiency with fire excess, sallow complexion indicates Spleen deficiency with dampness, purple-blue complexion indicates Qi stagnation and Blood stasis, dull complexion indicates Kidney essence depletion.


In traditional physiognomy, the face is divided into different regions called the "Twelve Palaces," including the Life Palace (glabella), Wealth Palace (nose tip), Siblings Palace (eyebrows), Marriage Palace (outer eye corners), Children Palace (under-eye area), Servants Palace (lower cheeks), Migration Palace (temple area), Illness Palace (nose bridge root), Career Palace (central forehead), Property Palace (between eyebrows), Fortune Palace (above eyebrow tails), and Parents Palace (forehead sides). The complexion, texture, and fullness of each palace are associated with different aspects of a person's fortune.


Those with a full Heavenly Court (forehead) enjoy smooth fortune in early life and are intelligent; those with a wide and bright Seal Hall (glabella) are broad-minded with strong benefactor luck; those with a high Mountain Root (nose bridge) have strong willpower and rich ancestral heritage; those with a round and fleshy nose tip enjoy prosperous wealth fortune and financial acumen; those with a deep and long Philtrum have longevity and flourishing descendants; those with a square and round Earth Cabinet (chin) enjoy both fortune and longevity in later life with stable family business. Conversely, those with a dark Seal Hall or horizontal lines face poor fortune recently and should be cautious; those with a low Mountain Root or horizontal lines have poor health and marital difficulties; those with premature Decree Lines (nasolabial folds) indicate a life of toil and labor.
